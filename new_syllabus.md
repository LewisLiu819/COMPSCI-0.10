# Detailed Elementary Computer Science Syllabus: "Coding Adventures"
*Adapted from UC Berkeley CS10 for Grades 4-6*

## Course Overview
**Duration:** 16 weeks (32 lessons, 2 sessions per week)  
**Class Time:** 50 minutes per session  
**Target Age:** Grades 4-6 (Ages 9-12)  
**Class Size:** 15-20 students  
**Prerequisites:** None

---

## UNIT 1: WHAT IS COMPUTER SCIENCE? (Weeks 1-2)

### Lesson 1.1: Welcome to the Digital World
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Introduction + Exploration Activity

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Define what a computer is in simple terms
- Identify at least 5 different types of computers in their environment
- Explain one way computers help people in their daily lives
- Demonstrate basic respect for classroom technology

#### Lesson Structure
**Opening Circle (10 minutes)**
- Welcome and introductions
- "What do you think of when you hear 'computer'?" brainstorm
- Share course expectations and classroom technology rules

**Main Activity: Computer Detective Hunt (25 minutes)**
- Students work in pairs with detective clipboards
- Explore classroom/school to find and document different computers
- Categories: Traditional computers, smart devices, embedded computers
- Recording sheet with pictures and descriptions

**Closing Discussion (10 minutes)**
- Share findings from detective hunt
- Create class chart of discovered computers
- Preview next lesson with excitement builder

**Technology Integration (5 minutes)**
- Brief introduction to classroom devices (tablets/computers)
- Practice proper handling and basic navigation

#### Materials Needed
- Detective clipboards with recording sheets
- Pencils and crayons
- Chart paper for class computer list
- Classroom devices for introduction
- Computer identification cards with pictures

#### Assessment
**Formative:** 
- Observation during detective hunt activity
- Participation in class discussions
- Completion of detective recording sheet

**Exit Ticket:** Draw one computer you found today and write one sentence about how it helps people.

#### Homework/Extension
Send home "Family Computer Hunt" worksheet for students to explore computers at home with families.

#### Vocabulary Introduced
- Computer
- Device
- Technology
- Smart (as in smartphone, smart TV)

### Lesson 1.2: Computers Are Everywhere!
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Investigation + Digital Exploration

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Categorize different types of computers by their functions
- Explain how computers process information (input → processing → output)
- Identify inputs and outputs for common devices
- Navigate basic tablet/computer interface

#### Lesson Structure
**Review and Share (10 minutes)**
- Students share family computer hunt discoveries
- Add new findings to class computer chart
- Celebrate diverse discoveries

**Input-Output Investigation (20 minutes)**
- Demonstrate input-output concept with classroom examples
- Students work in small groups to identify inputs/outputs for different devices
- Physical demonstration: students act as computers processing information
- Create input-output cards for matching game

**Digital Exploration Time (15 minutes)**
- Introduction to classroom tablets/computers
- Practice turning on devices, basic navigation
- Explore pre-loaded educational apps briefly
- Focus on observation rather than extensive use

**Wrap-up and Reflection (5 minutes)**
- Quick sharing: "One new thing I learned about computers"
- Preview of next lesson's exciting activities

#### Materials Needed
- Tablets or computers for student use
- Input-output investigation worksheets
- Device cards for matching activity
- Chart paper for ongoing class discoveries
- Simple props for human computer demonstration

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Input-output matching activity completion
- Observation of device handling and navigation
- Quality of explanations during investigations

**Summative:** Simple quiz with pictures - students identify inputs and outputs for 3 common devices.

#### Differentiation
- **Advanced learners:** Challenge to find unusual input-output examples
- **Struggling learners:** Provide pre-made input-output cards for reference
- **ELL students:** Use visual cards with minimal text, pair with bilingual buddy

#### Extension Activities
- Create input-output diagrams for devices at home
- Interview family members about how they use computers for work
- Start a class "Amazing Computer Facts" collection

#### Vocabulary Reinforced/Introduced
- Input
- Output
- Processing
- Interface
- Navigate

### Lesson 2.1: Thinking Like a Computer
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Unplugged Activity + Introduction to Algorithms

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Define "algorithm" in kid-friendly terms
- Write step-by-step instructions for a simple task
- Identify when instructions are too vague or missing steps
- Understand that computers need very precise instructions

#### Lesson Structure
**Human Robot Activity (20 minutes)**
- Teacher demonstrates being a "robot" following literal instructions
- Students give instructions for simple tasks (walk to the board, pick up a book)
- Highlight how robots/computers follow instructions exactly
- Students take turns being "robots" and "programmers"

**Algorithm Writing Workshop (20 minutes)**
- Introduce the term "algorithm" as a fancy word for step-by-step instructions
- Collaborative writing: Algorithm for brushing teeth
- Partner activity: Write algorithms for tying shoes or making a paper airplane
- Test algorithms by having partners follow them exactly

**Debugging Practice (8 minutes)**
- Present intentionally flawed algorithms
- Students identify missing or unclear steps
- Practice fixing (debugging) the instructions

**Reflection Circle (2 minutes)**
- "What was challenging about giving instructions to our human robots?"
- Connect to why programming requires precision

#### Materials Needed
- Large chart paper for algorithm writing
- Markers and pencils
- Simple props for robot activities (books, pencils, etc.)
- Pre-written flawed algorithms for debugging practice
- Algorithm template worksheets

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Observation during human robot activities
- Quality of algorithm writing (completeness, clarity)
- Participation in debugging exercises

**Performance Assessment:** Students successfully guide a partner through their written algorithm.

#### Cross-Curricular Connections
- **Math:** Sequencing and logical order
- **Language Arts:** Writing clear, detailed instructions
- **Life Skills:** Following directions and procedures

#### Homework Assignment
Write an algorithm for your morning routine. Test it by asking a family member to follow your instructions exactly.

#### Vocabulary Introduced
- Algorithm
- Instructions
- Steps
- Sequence
- Debug/Debugging

### Lesson 2.2: The Great Peanut Butter Sandwich Challenge
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Hands-on Algorithm Practice + Introduction to Programming

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Write detailed, unambiguous algorithms
- Recognize the importance of precision in instructions
- Connect algorithm writing to computer programming
- Demonstrate improved instruction-giving skills

#### Lesson Structure
**Setup and Challenge Introduction (10 minutes)**
- Review what algorithms are
- Present the challenge: Write instructions for making a peanut butter sandwich
- Emphasize that instructions will be followed EXACTLY
- Safety review for food activity

**Algorithm Writing Time (15 minutes)**
- Students work individually or in pairs
- Provide algorithm template for structure
- Encourage detailed, step-by-step thinking
- Teacher circulates to ask probing questions

**The Great Testing (20 minutes)**
- Teacher (or volunteer) follows several algorithms exactly as written
- Hilarious results when instructions are too vague
- Students observe and take notes on problems
- Celebrate both successes and learning opportunities

**Revision and Improvement (10 minutes)**
- Students revise their algorithms based on observations
- Partner sharing of improved versions
- Discuss what makes instructions clear vs. confusing

**Connection to Programming (5 minutes)**
- Explain how this connects to computer programming
- Preview upcoming lessons with programming tools
- Show simple example of programming blocks

#### Materials Needed
- Bread, peanut butter, jelly, plastic knives, paper plates
- Napkins and cleanup supplies
- Algorithm writing templates
- Chart paper for displaying successful algorithms
- Simple visual of programming blocks for preview

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Initial algorithm completeness
- Quality of observations during testing
- Improvement shown in algorithm revision

**Summative:** Compare original and revised algorithms using a simple rubric (clear steps, logical order, completeness).

#### Safety Considerations
- Check for food allergies before activity
- Use plastic knives only
- Have hand sanitizer available
- Prepare allergy-friendly alternatives (sunbutter, etc.)

#### Extension Activities
- Try the activity with other simple tasks (making a paper airplane, drawing a house)
- Create illustrated algorithm books for younger students
- Connect to family recipes and cooking instructions

#### Real-World Connections
- Cooking recipes as algorithms
- GPS directions as algorithms
- Assembly instructions for toys/furniture
- Emergency procedures as algorithms

#### Vocabulary Reinforced
- Algorithm
- Precise/Precision
- Instructions
- Debug
- Revise/Revision

## UNIT 2: BUILDING BLOCKS OF PROGRAMMING (Weeks 3-5)

### Lesson 3.1: Meet Scratch Jr. - Our Programming Playground
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Technology Introduction + Guided Exploration

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Navigate the Scratch Jr. interface confidently
- Identify and name basic programming blocks
- Create a simple sequence of 3-4 actions
- Explain what a "sprite" is in programming terms

#### Lesson Structure
**Technology Setup (10 minutes)**
- Distribute tablets/devices
- Practice proper device handling
- Log into Scratch Jr. accounts or start new projects
- Basic navigation review

**Guided Interface Tour (15 minutes)**
- Teacher demonstrates on projector/large screen
- Students follow along on their devices
- Identify key areas: stage, sprites, block palette, script area
- Practice selecting and dragging blocks

**First Programming Experience (20 minutes)**
- Challenge: Make your sprite move across the screen
- Students experiment with motion blocks
- Encourage trial and error exploration
- Teacher circulates providing individual support

**Sharing and Celebration (5 minutes)**
- Quick gallery walk to see classmates' projects
- Students share one discovery or challenge
- Save projects for next lesson

#### Materials Needed
- Tablets or computers with Scratch Jr. installed
- Projector/large screen for demonstrations
- Student device management system (numbers, charging station)
- Quick reference cards with basic blocks
- Success celebration stickers or stamps

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Observation of interface navigation skills
- Ability to drag and connect blocks
- Problem-solving persistence during exploration

**Quick Check:** Students demonstrate how to make their sprite move and explain which block they used.

#### Differentiation
- **Advanced learners:** Challenge to use multiple different motion blocks
- **Struggling learners:** Pair with tech-savvy classmate, provide visual step-by-step cards
- **Fine motor challenges:** Adjust device settings for easier touch response

#### Technology Troubleshooting Tips
- Prepare backup activities for technology failures
- Have student tech helpers identified
- Know basic restart/reset procedures
- Keep written instructions simple and visual

#### Vocabulary Introduced
- Sprite
- Block
- Script
- Stage
- Drag
- Programming

### Lesson 4.1: Choose Your Own Adventure - Introduction to Conditionals
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Interactive Storytelling + Programming Logic

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Understand "if-then" logic in everyday situations
- Identify conditions and responses in simple scenarios
- Use touch triggers to create interactive programs
- Create a simple branching story with 2-3 choices

#### Lesson Structure
**If-Then Logic Exploration (15 minutes)**
- Start with real-world examples: "If it's raining, then I bring an umbrella"
- Students generate more if-then examples from daily life
- Physical activity: Act out if-then scenarios
- Connect to decision-making and choices

**Interactive Story Planning (15 minutes)**
- Read a simple choose-your-own-adventure story together
- Analyze the decision points and outcomes
- Students plan their own simple interactive story
- Focus on one main character and 2-3 choices

**Programming Interactive Elements (15 minutes)**
- Demonstrate touch triggers in Scratch Jr.
- Students program their sprites to respond to touch
- Practice making different things happen based on user interaction
- Experiment with different responses (movement, sound, costume changes)

**Story Creation and Testing (5 minutes)**
- Begin creating interactive stories
- Partner testing - students try each other's programs
- Debugging and improvement based on testing

#### Materials Needed
- Sample choose-your-own-adventure books
- Story planning templates
- Devices with Scratch Jr.
- Chart paper for if-then examples
- Story idea prompt cards for inspiration

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Understanding demonstrated in if-then examples
- Quality of story planning
- Successful implementation of touch triggers
- Problem-solving during programming challenges

#### Literature Connection
- Read classic choose-your-own-adventure stories
- Analyze story structure and decision points
- Connect literary choices to programming logic

#### Real-World Applications
- Traffic light logic (if red, then stop)
- Thermostat operation (if cold, then heat)
- Game rules and consequences
- Safety procedures and responses

#### Vocabulary Introduced
- Conditional
- If-then
- Trigger
- Interactive
- Choice/Decision
- Response

### Lesson 4.2: Magic 8-Ball Fortune Teller
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Project-Based Learning + Logic Programming

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Program random responses to user input
- Understand how computers can simulate decision-making
- Create a complete interactive program from start to finish
- Debug programs that don't work as expected

#### Lesson Structure
**Introduction to Randomness (10 minutes)**
- Examine real Magic 8-Ball (if available) or show video
- Discuss how it creates different answers each time
- Brainstorm other examples of randomness in games and life
- Introduce the programming challenge

**Planning and Design (15 minutes)**
- Students design their fortune teller appearance
- Choose 6-8 different responses (positive, negative, neutral)
- Plan the user interaction (touch to get fortune)
- Sketch their design on paper first

**Programming Implementation (20 minutes)**
- Step-by-step programming of the Magic 8-Ball
- Use broadcast blocks for random responses
- Add visual and sound effects
- Teacher demonstrates, students follow with modifications

**Testing and Debugging (5 minutes)**
- Students test their programs extensively
- Partner testing for feedback
- Fix any problems discovered
- Celebrate working programs

#### Materials Needed
- Real Magic 8-Ball for demonstration (optional)
- Design planning sheets
- Devices with Scratch Jr.
- List of sample fortune responses
- Debugging checklist for students

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Quality of planning and design
- Successful implementation of random responses
- Persistence in debugging
- Creativity in personalizing the project

**Summative:** Completed, working Magic 8-Ball program that responds to user input with random messages.

#### Math Connection
- Basic probability concepts
- Understanding of random vs. predictable
- Counting and organizing response options

#### Extension Activities
- Add more sophisticated visual effects
- Create themed fortune tellers (sports, school, friendship)
- Program fortune tellers in different languages
- Create instruction cards for younger students

#### Debugging Skills Focus
- Check connections between blocks
- Test individual parts of the program
- Compare working and non-working versions
- Ask for help when stuck

#### Vocabulary Reinforced
- Random
- Broadcast
- Debug
- Test
- User input

### Lesson 5.1: Loop-de-Loop Patterns
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Pattern Exploration + Repetition Programming

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify patterns in nature, art, and daily life
- Understand how loops make programming more efficient
- Create repeating visual patterns using programming
- Use repeat blocks effectively in their projects

#### Lesson Structure
**Pattern Detective Hunt (15 minutes)**
- Show examples of patterns from nature, architecture, art
- Students identify what repeats in each pattern
- Physical pattern creation with manipulatives
- Connect patterns to the concept of loops in programming

**Introduction to Programming Loops (15 minutes)**
- Demonstrate inefficient vs. efficient programming
- Show how repeat blocks save time and space
- Practice with simple repeating movements
- Emphasize that loops are shortcuts for repetition

**Pattern Programming Workshop (15 minutes)**
- Students create geometric patterns using repeat blocks
- Focus on color, shape, and movement patterns
- Encourage experimentation with different repeat numbers
- Allow creative freedom within pattern constraints

**Gallery Walk and Analysis (5 minutes)**
- Display student patterns
- Identify the repeating elements in each creation
- Celebrate variety and creativity
- Discuss which patterns are most visually appealing and why

#### Materials Needed
- Pattern examples (printed images or slides)
- Manipulatives for physical pattern creation (blocks, shapes)
- Devices with Scratch Jr.
- Pattern planning worksheets
- Gallery display method (projection or sharing)

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Pattern recognition in examples
- Understanding of loop efficiency concept
- Successful use of repeat blocks
- Creativity and complexity of created patterns

#### Art Integration
- Study famous artists who used patterns (Mondrian, Escher)
- Create physical patterns with art supplies
- Discuss how patterns create visual interest
- Connect to cultural patterns from around the world

#### Math Connections
- Skip counting and multiplication concepts
- Geometric shape recognition
- Symmetry and transformation
- Basic algebraic thinking (pattern prediction)

#### Extension Activities
- Create patterns that grow or change over time
- Program patterns inspired by different cultures
- Make interactive patterns that respond to touch
- Design patterns for younger students to continue

#### Vocabulary Introduced
- Loop
- Repeat
- Pattern
- Efficient/Efficiency
- Geometric

## UNIT 3: PROBLEM SOLVING AND LOGIC (Weeks 6-8)

### Lesson 6.1: True or False Detective Agency
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Logic Games + Boolean Thinking

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Distinguish between true and false statements
- Apply logical reasoning to solve simple problems
- Understand that computers work with true/false logic
- Create programs that make decisions based on conditions

#### Lesson Structure
**Boolean Logic Introduction (12 minutes)**
- Play "True or False" with silly statements about the class
- Introduce Boolean as "computer true/false"
- Physical activity: Students move to "True" or "False" sides of room
- Connect to how computers make decisions

**Detective Logic Puzzles (18 minutes)**
- Present age-appropriate logic puzzles
- Students work in teams to solve mysteries using true/false clues
- Practice systematic thinking and elimination
- Celebrate different problem-solving approaches

**Programming Boolean Decisions (15 minutes)**
- Create simple programs that respond differently to true/false conditions
- Example: "If sprite is touched, then change color"
- Students experiment with different Boolean conditions
- Practice debugging when conditions don't work as expected

**Logic Challenge Reflection (5 minutes)**
- Share successful problem-solving strategies
- Discuss how breaking big problems into smaller true/false questions helps
- Preview upcoming programming challenges

#### Materials Needed
- True/False movement space in classroom
- Logic puzzle worksheets appropriate for grade level
- Devices with Scratch Jr.
- Detective badges or hats for engagement
- Chart paper for recording problem-solving strategies

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Accuracy in true/false categorization
- Quality of reasoning in logic puzzles
- Successful implementation of Boolean conditions in programming
- Participation in collaborative problem-solving

#### Critical Thinking Skills
- Systematic approach to problem-solving
- Evidence-based reasoning
- Logical deduction and elimination
- Recognition of patterns and relationships

#### Real-World Applications
- Scientific hypothesis testing
- Legal reasoning and evidence
- Medical diagnosis procedures
- Quality control and safety checks

#### Differentiation Strategies
- **Advanced learners:** Multi-step logic puzzles with complex conditions
- **Struggling learners:** Visual aids and manipulative supports for logic puzzles
- **ELL students:** Picture-based logic problems with minimal text

#### Vocabulary Introduced
- Boolean
- Logic
- Condition
- True/False
- Reasoning
- Evidence

### Lesson 7.1: Bug Busters - Debugging Workshop
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Problem-Solving Skills + Systematic Debugging

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify common programming errors systematically
- Apply debugging strategies independently
- Demonstrate persistence when facing programming challenges
- Help others debug their programs effectively

#### Lesson Structure
**Introduction to Bugs and Debugging (12 minutes)**
- Share the history of the term "bug" in computing (Grace Hopper story)
- Discuss how bugs are normal and expected in programming
- Introduce debugging as detective work
- Emphasize growth mindset: bugs are learning opportunities

**Debugging Strategy Training (18 minutes)**
- Teach systematic debugging approach: Stop, Look, Think, Try
- Practice with intentionally broken programs
- Demonstrate reading code step-by-step
- Practice asking good debugging questions

**Bug Hunt Challenge (15 minutes)**
- Students work with pre-made buggy programs
- Apply debugging strategies to fix various types of errors
- Categories: sequence errors, missing blocks, wrong parameters
- Celebrate successful fixes and learning from failures

**Peer Debugging Support (5 minutes)**
- Students help classmates debug their own projects
- Practice giving helpful feedback without just fixing for others
- Develop vocabulary for describing programming problems

#### Materials Needed
- Collection of intentionally buggy Scratch Jr. programs
- Debugging strategy posters and reference cards
- Devices with pre-loaded broken programs
- Debugging log sheets for tracking problem-solving
- Growth mindset posters and encouragement

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Application of systematic debugging strategies
- Persistence and positive attitude toward challenges
- Quality of help provided to classmates
- Improvement in independent problem-solving

#### Growth Mindset Focus
- Mistakes as learning opportunities
- Value of persistence and effort
- Celebrating progress and improvement
- Building confidence through successful problem-solving

#### Real-World Problem-Solving Skills
- Systematic approach to any type of problem
- Breaking complex problems into smaller parts
- Testing solutions and learning from results
- Collaborative problem-solving and help-seeking

#### Debugging Strategies Taught
1. **Stop:** Don't keep clicking randomly
2. **Look:** Examine the code carefully
3. **Think:** What should happen vs. what is happening?
4. **Try:** Make one change at a time and test

#### Extension Activities
- Create debugging challenges for other students
- Document common bugs and solutions
- Practice debugging in other areas (math problems, puzzles)
- Interview programmers about their debugging experiences

#### Vocabulary Introduced
- Bug
- Debugging
- Systematic
- Persistence
- Troubleshooting

### Lesson 7.2: Pair Programming Partnership
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Format:** Collaborative Programming + Communication Skills

#### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Work effectively with a programming partner
- Take turns as "driver" and "navigator" in pair programming
- Communicate technical ideas clearly to others
- Create better programs through collaboration than individual work

#### Lesson Structure
**Introduction to Pair Programming (10 minutes)**
- Explain roles: Driver (controls computer) and Navigator (guides and suggests)
- Demonstrate effective pair programming with student volunteer
- Establish ground rules for respectful collaboration
- Practice switching roles on cue

**Collaborative Programming Challenge (30 minutes)**
- Partners work together on progressively challenging tasks
- Start with simple tasks, build to complex projects
- Enforce role switching every 5-7 minutes
- Teacher circulates to coach collaboration skills

**Reflection and Improvement (7 minutes)**
- Partners discuss what worked well and what was challenging
- Share effective collaboration strategies with class
- Set goals for future pair programming sessions

**Celebration of Partnership (3 minutes)**
- Recognize successful collaboration examples
- Acknowledge different types of contributions
- Thank partners for their teamwork

#### Materials Needed
- Devices with Scratch Jr. (one per pair)
- Pair programming role cards
- Timer for role switching
- Collaboration challenge tasks
- Reflection worksheets

#### Assessment
**Formative:**
- Quality of collaboration and communication
- Effective use of driver/navigator roles
- Willingness to share ideas and accept suggestions
- Improvement in programming outcomes through partnership

#### Social-Emotional Learning
- Turn-taking and sharing
- Active listening skills
- Giving and receiving feedback
- Conflict resolution and compromise

#### Communication Skills Development
- Explaining technical concepts clearly

## CONCLUSION AND COURSE IMPACT

This comprehensive elementary computer science curriculum provides students with a solid foundation in computational thinking, programming skills, and digital citizenship while fostering creativity, collaboration, and confidence. By adapting the rigorous content of UC Berkeley's CS10 course for elementary learners, students gain early exposure to computer science concepts that will serve them well in middle school, high school, and beyond.

The course emphasizes hands-on learning, creative expression, and real-world application, ensuring that students not only learn technical skills but also understand how computer science can be used to solve problems, create art, tell stories, and make positive impacts in their communities. Through project-based learning, collaborative activities, and regular reflection, students develop both technical competencies and essential 21st-century skills.

Most importantly, this curriculum aims to develop positive associations with computer science learning, building confidence and enthusiasm that will encourage students to continue their exploration of technology and programming throughout their educational journey and into potential technology careers. By providing all students with access to high-quality computer science education at the elementary level, we can help ensure that the next generation of technologists, innovators, and digital citizens reflects the full diversity of our communities and is prepared to tackle the challenges and opportunities of our increasingly digital world.
